  <!-- Intro -->
  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    I am <strong>Nima Taghipour Bazargani</strong>, an electrical engineer and researcher with a focus on
    <strong>power systems</strong>, <strong>machine learning</strong>, and
    <strong>networked dynamical systems</strong>. My work is motivated by a simple question:
    how do large infrastructures behave when viewed as interacting physical, informational, and economic layers,
    and how can we design tools that make these systems more observable, understandable, and resilient in practice?
  </p>

  <hr>

  <!-- Professional Experience -->
  <h2>Professional Experience</h2>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Over the past years I have worked on real-time event identification, wide-area measurement analytics,
    forecasting frameworks for system strength and inertia, and large-scale electricity market studies.
    This path has taken me through academic research labs, industry roles, and international collaborations,
    and it has shaped a perspective that connects theoretical models with operational realities in power
    and energy systems.
  </p>

  <!-- Image -->
  <div style="width:100%; display:flex; justify-content:center; margin:1.2rem 0;">
    <img src="/images/world-network-1.jpg"
         alt="Global network visualization"
         style="width:600px; max-width:100%; border-radius:8px; box-shadow:0 8px 24px rgba(0,0,0,0.45);">
  </div>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Most recently, I worked as a <strong>Power System Engineer at
    <a href="https://www.electricpowergroup.com/" target="_blank">Electric Power Group (EPG)</a></strong> in Pasadena,
    California. In this role, I developed a real-time <strong>inertia and system-strength forecasting framework</strong>
    that integrates synchrophasor data, physics-informed features, and continual-learning techniques. The work involved
    building a robust Python pipeline for streaming data ingestion, adaptive model updates, and multi-horizon forecasting,
    with results incorporated into operator-facing visualization tools that enhance situational awareness for utilities
    and reliability coordinators.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Before joining EPG, I served as a <strong>Network Model Analyst at
    <a href="https://mcgillstlaurent.com/cwp-energy/" target="_blank">CWP Energy</a></strong>, collaborating remotely
    with teams in Montreal. I conducted large-scale <strong>security-constrained unit commitment</strong> and
    <strong>security-constrained economic dispatch</strong> simulations across major U.S. energy marketsâ€”including MISO,
    SPP, PJM, and ERCOT. I developed and maintained a <strong>Python- and Google Cloudâ€“based</strong> simulation framework
    for long-term scenario evaluation, congestion analysis, and strategy assessment, strengthening my understanding of how
    network topology and constraints shape pricing, risk, and market behavior.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    As a <strong>Data Scientist Intern at
    <a href="https://drivepowerline.com/" target="_blank">Powerline (gigElev)</a></strong>, I contributed to the development
    and refinement of software tools for predicting electricity-grid and e-mobility features. My work focused on
    Python-based integrations with third-party APIs, supporting automated tariff and rate calculations, and contributing to
    data preprocessing, feature analysis, and machine-learning components within Powerlineâ€™s product ecosystem.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    I also contributed to collaborative R&amp;D through the
    <strong><a href="https://us-isr-energycenter.org/energy_cyber/USIE.html" target="_blank">
    U.S.â€“Israel Energy Center (BIRD Foundation)</a></strong>, where I helped develop an open-source
    <strong>event-identification package</strong> for synchrophasor data. My work included feature-extraction modules,
    data-generation workflows, and semi-supervised learning components for disturbance classification.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Earlier in my career, I worked at
    <strong><a href="https://www.maki.tu-darmstadt.de/forschung_maki/index.en.jsp" target="_blank">
    TU Darmstadt (MAKI CRC 1053)</a></strong> in Germany on <strong>virtual network embedding</strong> for cyber-physical
    infrastructures, developing stochastic mixed-integer optimization models focused on resilience and efficient resource
    allocation.
  </p>

  <hr>

  <!-- Academic Journey -->
  <h2>Academic Journey</h2>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    I completed my Ph.D. in Electrical Engineering at Arizona State University (ASU), specializing in the real-time
    identification of power system events using high-dimensional spatio-temporal PMU data, under the guidance of
    <a href="https://sites.google.com/site/okosut/">Professor Oliver Kosut</a> and
    <a href="https://sankar.engineering.asu.edu/">Professor Lalitha Sankar</a>.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    During my doctoral studies, I contributed to several large research efforts, including NSF- and PSERC-funded projects
    on high-dimensional data science, information-theoretic monitoring, and event identification, as well as the
    U.S.â€“Israel Energy Center program on ML-based frameworks for PMU event identification.
  </p>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Before ASU, I earned my <strong>M.Sc. in Power Systems</strong> from
    <strong>K.N. Toosi University of Technology</strong>, graduating second in my program. My research focused on microgrid
    resiliency and optimal sizing of battery storage systems. I completed my <strong>B.Sc. in Electrical Engineering</strong>
    at <strong>Shahid Beheshti University</strong>, where I studied transmission and distribution systems along with
    transformer testing infrastructure.
  </p>

  <hr>

  <!-- Research and Technical Interests -->
  <h2>Research and Technical Interests</h2>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    My research interests are centered on <strong>networked dynamical systems</strong> in power and energy contexts.
    I am particularly interested in:
  </p>

  <ul style="text-align:justify; padding-left:1.4rem;">
    <li>how graph structure and network topology influence system dynamics and risk;</li>
    <li>how physical, cyber, and market layers interact in real operating environments;</li>
    <li>how measurements and models can be combined into robust monitoring and decision tools;</li>
    <li>how learning algorithms can adapt to evolving system conditions and rare events.</li>
  </ul>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    These themes have gradually evolved into a broader direction that I refer to as
    <strong>Graph Dynamical Flows</strong>, which aims to organize thinking about flows, constraints, and feedback across
    interconnected physical and informational networks.
  </p>

  <hr>

  <!-- Personal Interests -->
  <h2>Personal Interests</h2>

  <p style="text-align:justify;">
    Outside engineering, I enjoy exploring <strong>neuroscience</strong>, <strong>psychology</strong>, <strong>history</strong>,
    and <strong>philosophy</strong>, especially where they intersect with questions of systems and human behavior. I also enjoy
    hiking, camping, off-road travel, and designing or solving mechanical and 3D puzzles. These interests mirror my professional
    curiosity about structure, complexity, and how different parts of a system fit and move together.
  </p>
